Best Answer - Chosen by Voters

I had turned 13 5 days previous & had just started high school. I didn't even hear of the events of that day until I got home from school, my grandmother, whom I live with, was watching it on the news & to begin with I thought it was some sort of movie preview. I'd never even heard of the World Trade Centre. The second she told me it was a terrorist attack on these monumental buildings in America, despite the fact that I'd not known of them until now, my heart felt like it'd hit the floor & I felt nothing but fear & sorrow for the people involved. My mind went blank for a few seconds & I sat down just starring at the tv as I wondered why the hell anyone would do such a thing.

Even though I knew nobody directly involved in the attack, I often find myself thinking about those who were affected & I still find it impossible to comprehend what they've had to experience.

    I was in the Allstate building in Roanoke, VA, which was a stop on the vending route job I had at the time.     Later, as I was finishing up there and getting ready to leave, I came around a hallway corner with my cart, and a crowd of people was gathered around a wall mounted television. I stopped to see what it was that had everyone's undivided attention and received the news that the Pentagon had been hit as well. I remember realizing at that point that whatever was happening wasn't just a random thing, but was something intentional and coordinated. At that point, I became frightened and wondered how many other places were going to be attacked before it was all over.

    After that, I spent days upon days glued to the radio and television at every available opportunity, trying to find out anything I could.

    For me, it started with getting ready for work like any other day. In the early morning, I didn't have any radio or TV on until I got into the car to drive to work (a little before 7:50 am my time, 9:50 am Eastern). My radio was on a station where the morning DJs had a reputation for being practical jokers, so when they said something like "If you're planning to fly anywhere today, forget it, because all flights going anywhere have been grounded", I thought they were up to their usual pranks. That is, until a special report cut in and said that the World Trade Towers were on fire and the Pentagon had been hit by an airplane. The same report also said that there had been a car bomb at the State Department, which we now know didn't happen.

    The South Tower collapsed right after I got to work. I dashed back home (about a ten-minute drive), grabbed a portable TV, went back to work and plugged it in at my office. Not long after that, the North Twoer collapsed. We all know how the day went after that. At work, absolutely NOTHING was happening as everyone was glued to whatever TV they could find. There were also next to no clients coming in (I was working at a non-profit human services agency).

    My main memories from the day are 1) Thinking at first that the whole thing was a prank and 2) How everything just seemed to stop for the rest of the day. Alan Jackson's song about 9-11 starts off with the lyric "Where were you when the world stopped turning?" That's about the best way you can put it.

    Also, later I asked my grandma (who was born in 1922 and in 1941 was a student nurse) which was the bigger shock: Pearl Harbor or 9-11. Without hesitation, she said 9-11 because while Pearl Harbor was considered a cushy backwater posting, the military personnel there at least had it in the back of their minds that they could be in the line of fire - particularly since Europe was already at war. 9-11 came out of nowhere and hit people who had no inkling that there was even a possibility of anything so drastic happening, so in Grandma's view 9-11 was the bigger shock.

    September 11 attacks
    The September 11 attacks (also referred to as September 11, September 11th or 9/11[nb 1]) were a series of four coordinated suicide attacks upon the United States in New York City and the Washington, D.C., area on September 11, 2001. On that Tuesday morning, 19 terrorists from the Islamist militant group al-Qaeda hijacked four passenger jets. The hijackers intentionally crashed two planes, American Airlines Flight 11 and United Airlines Flight 175, into the Twin Towers of the World Trade Center in New York City; both towers collapsed within two hours. Hijackers crashed American Airlines Flight 77 into the Pentagon in Arlington, Virginia. The fourth jet, United Airlines Flight 93, crashed into a field near Shanksville, Pennsylvania, after passengers attempted to take control before it could reach the hijacker's intended target in Washington, D.C. Nearly 3,000 died in the attacks.
